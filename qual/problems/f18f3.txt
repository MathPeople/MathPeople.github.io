<problem><p>Calculate the integral</p><p>\[\int_\gamma\frac{dz}{z^2+\pi^2}\]</p><p>where \(\gamma(\theta)=\theta e^{i\theta}\) for \(\theta\in(0,2\pi)\).</p></problem><solution><p>First, let&apos;s close \(\gamma\) with the curve \(c(t)=t\) for \(t\in(0,2\pi)\). Then the concatenation \(p\) of \(\gamma\) with the reverse of \(c\) \((p=\gamma-c)\) is a closed loop. The poles of \(\frac{1}{z^2+\pi^2}\) occur at \(\pm\pi i\), we need to check if these points are encircled by \(p\). For all \(\theta\in(0,2\pi), |\gamma(\theta)|=\theta\). Then \(\pi i\) is not encircled but \(-\pi i\) is. We apply the Cauchy integral formula to get</p><p>\[\int_p\frac{\frac{1}{z-\pi i}}{z+\pi i}dz=2\pi i\left(\frac{1}{-\pi i-\pi i}\right)=-1\]</p><p> This helps because </p><p>\[\int_pf(z)dz=\int_\gamma f(z)dz-\int_cf(z)dz\]</p><p> Therefore </p><p>\[\int_\gamma f(z)dz=\int_cf(z)dz-1\]</p><p> Now to compute the integral over \(c\). Recall \(c(t)=t\), so the integral parametrizes as </p><p>\[\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{dt}{t^2+\pi^2}\]</p><p> This is an arctangent integral, the answer is \(\frac{\arctan(2)}{\pi}\). Thus our desired integral is </p><p>\[\int_\gamma\frac{dz}{z^2+\pi^2}=\frac{\arctan(2)}{\pi}-1\]</p></solution><topics><CauchyIntegralFormula/></topics><instructors><c/></instructors>
