<problem><p>Let \(p\) be a polynomial of degree \(d\geq2\) with distinct roots \(a_i\). Show</p><p>\[\sum_{i=1}^d\frac{1}{p&apos;(a_i)}=0\]</p></problem><solution><p>First, are we dividing by zero? How do we know that \(p&apos;(a_i)\neq0\) for all \(i\)? This is because the roots are distinct. If \(p&apos;(a)=0\) then \(a\) is a root of order at least 2, so it is not a distinct root. Therefore \(p&apos;(a_i)\neq0\) for all \(i\). Moreover, note \(p\) takes the form</p><p>\[p(z)=\lambda\prod_{i=1}^n(z-a_i)\]</p><p>by the fundamental theorem of algebra. Now to answer the question. Let \(R&gt;0\) be large enough that all \(|a_i|&lt;R\) and let \(\gamma(t)=Re^{it}\). Consider the integral </p><p>\[\int_\gamma\prod_{i=1}^n\frac{1}{z-a_i}dz\]</p><p> Fix any \(i\) and use Cauchy&apos;s integral formula for the curve \(\gamma_i\) which encircles only the pole at \(a_i\). </p><p>\[\int_{\gamma_i}\frac{\prod_{j\neq i}\frac{1}{z-a_j}}{z-a_i}dz=(2\pi i)\prod_{j\neq i}\frac{1}{a_i-a_j}=(2\pi i)\frac{\lambda}{p&apos;(a_i)}\]</p><p> The last equality comes from the multi-chain-rule computation of \(p&apos;\), plugging in \(a_i\) cancels most of the terms. Therefore </p><p>\[\int_\gamma\prod_{i=1}^n\frac{1}{z-a_i}dz=(2\pi i)\sum_{i=1}^n\frac{\lambda}{p&apos;(a_i)}\]</p><p> Alternately, let \(d\) be the degree of \(p\). Then \(|\prod_{i=1}^n(z-a_i)|\sim R^d\) as \(|z|\) gets very large. Then the integral is </p><p>\[\left|\int_\gamma\prod_{i=1}^n\frac{1}{z-a_i}dz\right|\sim\left|\int_\gamma\frac{1}{R^d}dz\right|\leq2\pi\frac{1}{R^{d-1}}\to0\]</p><p> The limit goes to 0 because \(d\geq2\). That shows our result.</p></solution><topics><IntegralEstimates/><Degree/><ResidueTheorem/><CauchyIntegralFormula/><EntireFunction/><FundamentalTheoremOfAlgebra/></topics><instructors><a/><b/></instructors>
