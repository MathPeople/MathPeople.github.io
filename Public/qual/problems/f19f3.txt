<problem><p>Assume that \(f\) and \(g\) are analytic near a point \(a\in\mathbb{C}\) with \(f(a)=g(a)=0\) and \(f&apos;(a),g&apos;(a)\neq0\). Prove that</p><p>\[\lim_{z\to a}\frac{f(z)}{g(z)}=\lim_{z\to a}\frac{f&apos;(z)}{g&apos;(z)}=\frac{f&apos;(a)}{g&apos;(a)}.\]</p></problem><solution><p>We know that, as an analytic function which takes \(a\) to 0, \(f(z)=(z-a)^kr(z)\) where \(k\geq1\) and \(r\) is analytic. Moreover, \(k\) must be 1 and \(r(a)\neq0\) because \(f&apos;(a)\neq0\), so \(f(z)=(z-a)r(z)\). Likewise \(g(z)=(z-a)s(z)\) for some analytic \(s\) with \(s(a)\neq0\). Then</p><p>\[\lim_{z\to a}\frac{f(z)}{g(z)}=\lim_{z\to a}\frac{(z-a)r(z)}{(z-a)s(z)}=\lim_{z\to a}\frac{r(z)}{s(z)}=\frac{r(a)}{s(a)}\]</p><p>Also \(f&apos;(z)=r(z)+(z-a)r&apos;(z)\) and \(g&apos;(z)=s(z)+(z-a)s&apos;(z)\), so \(f&apos;(a)=r(a), g&apos;(a)=s(a)\), and</p><p>\[\lim_{z\to a}\frac{f&apos;(z)}{g&apos;(z)}=\lim_{z\to a}\frac{r(z)+(z-a)r&apos;(z)}{s(z)+(z-a)s&apos;(z)}\]</p><p>Now \(r&apos;\) and \(s&apos;\) are analytic so, in particular, are continuous, and \(r(a)\) and \(s(a)\) are still nonzero, so this limit reduces to \(\frac{r(a)}{s(a)}=\frac{f&apos;(a)}{g&apos;(a)}\) also.</p></solution><topics><Degree/></topics><instructors><d/></instructors>
