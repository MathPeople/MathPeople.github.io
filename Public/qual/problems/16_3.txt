<problem><p>Suppose that the radius of convergence of</p><p>\[f(z)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_nz^n\]</p><p>is R. Prove the power series</p><p>\[\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{1}{n+1}a_nz^{n+1}\]</p><p>has the same radius of convergence \(R\), and is actually the derivative of \(f\).</p></problem><solution><p>Recall the radius of convergence \(R\) is given by the root test,</p><p>\[\frac{1}{R}=\limsup_{n\to\infty}\sqrt[n]{a_n}\]</p><p>To show the new radius is the same, we have to show </p><p>\[\limsup_{n\to\infty}\sqrt[n]{a_n}=\limsup_{n\to\infty}\sqrt[n]{\frac{a_n}{n+1}}\]</p><p> However, we know \(\limsup\sqrt[n]{a_n}=\frac{1}{R}\). Then all we have to show is that \(\limsup\sqrt[n]{n+1}=1\). This is easy enough, </p><p>\[\lim\sqrt[n]{n+1}=\lim e^{\frac{\ln(n+1)}{n}}=\exp\left(\lim\frac{\ln(n+1)}{n}\right)=\exp\left(\lim\frac{\frac{1}{n+1}}{1}\right)=e^0=1\]</p><p> So we have shown that the radius of convergence is the same. Moreover, let \(S_n\) be the \(n^{th}\) partial sum for \(f\). The derivative of \(S_n\) is the \(n+1^{th}\) partial sum of the series in question, call it \(S&apos;_n\). Because our convergence is uniform (on compact sets), we can swap limits around. Then </p><p>\[f&apos;(z_0)=\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}=\lim_{z\to z_0}\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{S_n(z)-S_n(z_0)}{z-z_0}=\lim_{n\to\infty}\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{S_n(z)-S_n(z_0)}{z-z_0}=\lim_{n\to\infty}S&apos;_n(z_0)\]</p></solution><topics><RadiusOfConvergence/><SumConvergence/></topics><instructors><a/><c/></instructors>
