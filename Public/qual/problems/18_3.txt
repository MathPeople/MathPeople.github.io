<problem><p>Use the residue theorem to find</p><p>\[\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{1}{(2+\sin\theta)^2}d\theta\]</p></problem><solution><p>Recall Euler&apos;s formula \(e^{i\theta}=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta\) gives </p><p>\[\sin\theta=\frac{1}{2i}\left(e^{i\theta}-e^{-i\theta}\right)\]</p><p>Let \(z=e^{i\theta}\). This turns \((0,2\pi)\) into the unit circle \(C\) and \(dz=ie^{i\theta}d\theta\), or \(-i\frac{dz}{z}=d\theta\). Also \(\sin(\theta)=\frac{1}{2i}\left(z-\frac{1}{z}\right)\). Thus our integral is </p><p>\[\int_C\frac{-i\frac{dz}{z}}{\left(2+\frac{1}{2i}\left(z-\frac{1}{z}\right)\right)^2}\]</p><p> Multiplying top and bottom by \((2iz)^2=-4z^2\) gives </p><p>\[\int_C\frac{4izdz}{(4iz+z^2-1)^2}=\int_C\frac{4izdz}{(z^2+4iz-1)^2}\]</p><p> The roots of \(z^2+4iz-1\) are at \(\frac{1}{2}(-4i\pm\sqrt{-16+4})=\frac{1}{2}(-4\pm2\sqrt3 i)=-2\pm\sqrt3i\). Let \(a=(-2+\sqrt3)i\) and \(b=(-2-\sqrt3)i\). Only \(a\) is in the unit disc. </p><p> Recall the residue formula for poles, if \(f\) has a pole of order \(m\) at \(a\), so \(f(z)=\frac{g(z)}{(z-a)^m}\), then </p><p>\[Res(f;a)=\frac{1}{(m-1)!}g^{(m-1)}(a)\]</p><p> In our situation, \(f(z)=\frac{4iz}{(z-a)^2(z-b)^2}\) so \(g(z)=\frac{4iz}{(z-b)^2}\). Then the residue at \(a\) is </p><p>\[g&apos;(a)=\frac{4i(a-b)^2-8i(a-b)a}{(a-b)^4}=4i\frac{(2\sqrt3i)^2-2(2\sqrt3i)(-2+\sqrt3)i}{(2\sqrt3i)^4}\]</p><p> </p><p>\[=4i\frac{-12+4\sqrt3(-2+\sqrt3)}{144}=16i\frac{-2\sqrt3}{144}=\frac{-2\sqrt3i}{9}\]</p><p> Thus the integral is </p><p>\[\int_Cf(z)dz=2\pi iRes(f;a)=2\pi i\frac{-2\sqrt3i}{9}=\frac{4\sqrt3\pi}{9}\]</p></solution><topics><ExpAndTrig/><RealIntegral/><ResidueTheorem/></topics><instructors><a/><b/></instructors>
