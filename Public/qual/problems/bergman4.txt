<problem><p>Show that \(\{z^n\}_{n=0}^\infty\) forms an orthogonal basis of \(L^2(\mathbb{D})\). Normalize it and compute \(K(z,z)\) on \(\mathbb{D}\). You should wind up with a power series in \(|z|\), compute its radius of convergence.</p></problem><solution><p>First, it forms a basis because holomorphic functions are analytic and this is essentially the definition of analytic, what with power series and all. Let \(n,m\in\mathbb{N}\). We will compute \(\langle z^n,z^m\rangle\) with the use of polar coordinates. Recall \(dxdy=rdrd\theta\).</p><p>\[\langle z^n,z^m\rangle=\iint_\mathbb{D}z^n\overline{z^m}dxdy=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1r^{n+m+1}e^{i\theta(n-m)}drd\theta=\frac{1}{n+m+2}\int_0^{2\pi}e^{i\theta(n-m)}d\theta\]</p><p>If \(n\neq m\) then the remaining integral vanishes, being over a closed loop for a function with an antiderivative. If \(n=m\) then we obtain</p><p>\[\langle z^n,z^n\rangle=\frac{2\pi}{2n+2}\]</p><p>Thus the sequence \(\left\{\sqrt{\frac{2n+2}{2\pi}}z^n\right\}\) is an orthonormal basis of \(L^2(\mathbb{D})\). Using our formula for \(K\),</p><p>\[K(z,z)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{2n+2}{2\pi}|z|^{2n}\]</p><p>Let&apos;s rewrite this using \(m=2n\) to</p><p>\[\sum_{m=0}^\infty\frac{m+2}{2\pi}|z|^m\]</p><p>We can do the ratio test after this index change to compute the radius of convergence,</p><p>\[\lim_{m\to\infty}\frac{m+3}{2\pi}\frac{2\pi}{m+2}=1\]</p></solution><topics><RealIntegral/><SequenceOfFunctions/><SumConvergence/><RadiusOfConvergence/><CauchyIntegralTheorem/></topics><instructors><e/></instructors>
