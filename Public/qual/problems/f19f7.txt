<problem><p>Let</p><p>\[f(z)=\frac{1}{z(z-1)(z-2)}.\]</p><p>Give the Laurent expansion of \(f(z)\) in the annulus \(\text{ann}_{1,2}(0):=\mathbb{C}\setminus\overline B_2(0)\).</p></problem><solution><p>We can use partial fractions to write \(\frac{1}{z(z-1)(z-2)}\) as \(\frac{a}{z}+\frac{b}{z-1}+\frac{c}{z-2}\). For completeness we do that here. Cross multiplying gives</p><p>\[1=a(z-1)(z-2)+bz(z-2)+cz(z-1)\]</p><p>At \(z=0\) we find \(a=\frac{1}{2}\), at \(z=1\) we find \(b=-1\), and at \(z=2\) we find \(c=\frac{1}{2}\). Thus</p><p>\[f(z)=\frac{1}{2z}-\frac{1}{z-1}+\frac{1}{2(z-2)}\]</p><p>The region in which we are interested is outside all of the poles, so we will be factoring \(z\) out of the denominator as much as we can. Thus we are more interested in expressing \(f\) as</p><p>\[f(z)=\frac{1}{2z}-\frac{1}{z(1-\frac{1}{z})}+\frac{1}{2z(1-\frac{2}{z})}\]</p><p>Using the geometric series formula on the second term (where \(|\frac{1}{z}|&lt;1\) because \(|z|&gt;1\)) and the third (where \(|\frac{2}{z}|&lt;1\) because \(|z|&gt;2\)), we have</p><p>\[f(z)=\frac{1}{2z}-\frac{1}{z}\sum_{n=0}^\infty\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)^n+\frac{1}{2z}\sum_{n=0}^\infty\left(\frac{2}{z}\right)^n\]</p><p>\[=\frac{1}{2}z^{-1}-\sum_{n=0}^\infty z^{-n-1}+\sum_{n=0}^\infty2^{n-1}z^{-n-1}=\frac{1}{2}z^{-1}+\sum_{n=1}^\infty(2^{n-2}-1)z^{-n}\]</p></solution><topics><SumConvergence/><RadiusOfConvergence/><Laurent/></topics><instructors><d/></instructors>
