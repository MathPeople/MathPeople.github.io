<problem><p>Show that \(f(z)=|z|^2\) has a derivative only at the origin.</p></problem><solution><p>First we rewrite \(f\) as \(f(z)=z\overline z\). The difference quotient is</p><p>\[\lim_{h\to0}\frac{(z+h)\overline{(z+h)}-z\overline z}{h}=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{z\overline h+h\overline z+h\overline h}{h}\]</p><p>The \(h\) cancels on the last two terms, giving</p><p>\[=z\left(\lim_{h\to0}\frac{\overline h}{h}\right)+\overline z+\lim_{h\to0}\overline h=z\left(\lim_{h\to0}\frac{(\overline h)}{h}\right)+\overline z\]</p><p>As we all know, \(\lim_{h\to0}\frac{(\overline h)}{h}\) is ill-defined, we can see this by taking \(h=\alpha t\) for \(\alpha=1,i\) and taking \(t\to 0\). The ill-definedness persists precisely when \(z\neq0\); if \(z=0\) the problem doesn&apos;t matter because the 0 annihilates the numerator. Thus \(f\) has a derivative only at \(z=0\).</p></solution><topics><Conjugation/><ElementaryDefinitions/></topics><instructors><d/></instructors>
