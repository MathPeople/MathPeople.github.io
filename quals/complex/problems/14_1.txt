<problem><p>Using contour integration, find the value of</p><p>\[\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{dt}{\cos(t)-2}\]</p></problem><solution><p>Recall Euler&apos;s formula \(e^{it}=\cos(t)+i\sin(t)\). From this we get the identity</p><p>\[\cos(t)=\frac{e^{it}+e^{-it}}{2}\]</p><p>Then we can rewrite our integral as</p><p>\[\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{dt}{\cos(t)-2}=\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{dt}{\frac{e^{it}+e^{-it}}{2}-2}=\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{2dt}{e^{it}+e^{-it}-4}=\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{2e^{it}dt}{e^{2it}+1-4e^{it}}\]</p><p>Now we make the substitution \(z=e^{it}\), so the path \(\gamma\) becomes the unit circle, to obtain</p><p>\[\int_\gamma\frac{\frac{2}{i}dz}{z^2+1-4z}=-2i\int_\gamma\frac{dz}{z^2-4z+1}\]</p><p>The poles of this integrand occur at \(z=\frac{1}{2}(4\pm\sqrt{16-4})=2\pm\sqrt{3}\). Only \(2-\sqrt 3\) is encircled by \(\gamma\), so we use Cauchy&apos;s integral formula to obtain</p><p>\[\int_\gamma\frac{1/(z-(2+\sqrt 3))}{z-(2-\sqrt 3)}dz=2\pi i\frac{1}{2-\sqrt 3-2-\sqrt 3}=\frac{\pi i}{-\sqrt 3}\]</p><p>Considering the \(-2i\) coefficient from earlier gives us that our answer is</p><p>\[\frac{-2\pi}{\sqrt 3}\]</p></solution><topics><RealIntegral/><ResidueTheorem/><CauchyIntegralFormula/><ExpAndTrig/></topics><instructors><a/><b/></instructors>
