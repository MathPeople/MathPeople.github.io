Problem of the Week
We know from single variable real calculus that \(\ln|x|\) is not continuous on \((-1,1)\). How smooth is \(x^n\ln|x|\) on \((-1,1)\) for \(n\in\mathbb{N}\)? Is there a (not constantly zero) \(C^\infty\) smooth function \(f\) on the disc such that the product \(f(x)\ln|x|\) is \(C^\infty\) smooth on \((-1,1)\)? Is there a (not constantly zero) analytic function \(g\) on \((-1,1)\) such that the product \(g(x)\ln|x|\) is analytic on \((-1,1)\)?
Construct an analytic function on the disc which does not extend analytically beyond any boundary point. Can you do so in such a way that the function is continuous on the closed unit disc? Bonus: Can you find such a function which is smooth on the boundary?
Is \(\mathbb{D}\setminus\{0\}\) biholomorphic to \(\big((-1,1)\times(-1,1)\big)\setminus\{0\}\)? What about \(\mathbb{D}\setminus\{a\}\) and \(\big((-1,1)\times(-1,1)\big)\setminus\{b\}\) for any \(a\in\mathbb{D}\) and \(b\in(-1,1)\times(-1,1)\)? Same question but with pairs instead of single points for \(a\) and \(b\), is the disc minus any two points biholomorphic to the square minus any two points?

Let \(f\) be an entire function. We say \(f\) extends to the sphere if there is a holomorphic function \(F:S^2\to S^2\) such that \(F|_\mathbb{C}=f\). Prove that \(f\) extends to the sphere if and only if

\[\lim_{|z|\to\infty}\frac{1}{f(z)}\]

exists. Show that every polynomial extends to the sphere.

On a to-be-related note, let \(g,h:\mathbb{D}\to\mathbb{C}\) be holomorphic such that \(g(0)=h(0)=0\). Let \(n_g\leq n_h\) be the orders of the roots of \(g,h\) at \(0\). Clearly \(g+h\) has a root at 0, what is its order?

Use the above two results and the degree argument to prove the fundamental theorem of algebra.

Let \(f_n\) be a sequence of entire functions, each of which extends to the sphere. Suppose \(f_n\to f\) uniformly on compact sets. Is \(f\) entire? Does \(f\) extend to the sphere? Describe which entire functions extend to the sphere and provide an entire function which does not.

Prove this statement which can be taken from the proof of theorem 3.7 in Conway: Let \(f:\mathbb{D}\to\mathbb{C}\) be a holomorphic function such that \(f(0)=0\). Then there is an \(n\geq0\) such that \[f(z)=z^ng(z)\] and either \(g\) is constant or there is an \(r>0\) such that \(g:B_r(0)\to g(B_r(0))\) is a biholomorphism. Here \(B_r(0)=\{z:|z|<r\}\) and \(\mathbb{D}=B_1(0)\). Find \(n, g\), and \(r\) for \(f\) chosen from the functions below: \[z, z^2+z, \sin(z), \frac{1}{z+1}-1, \ln(z+1), \cos(\cos(z)-1)-1\] This result (which I call the local mapping theorem, coordinate-free version) essentially proves the uniqueness principle (Conway 3.7), the maximum modulus principle, and the open mapping theorem, and the local mapping theorem which clarifies several statements and proofs in part C.\[\]For extra credit, prove the local mapping theorem: If \(M,N\) are Riemann surfaces, \(f:M\to N\) is holomorphic, and \(x\in M, y\in N\) with \(f(x)=y\), then there are charts centered at \(x\) and \(y\) such that the coordinate version of \(f\) is the function \(z^{d_x}\) for some integer \(d_x\geq0\). Essentially this proof is just playing with charts and applying the statement above.\[\]Further, show that \(d_x\) is well-defined, i.e. \(d_x\) can depend on \(x\) but not on the charts chosen.
We all know the Pythagorean identity \(\sin^2(\theta)+\cos^2(\theta)=1\) for \(\theta\in\mathbb{R}\). We construct the unit circle as the set \[\big\{(\sin(\theta),\cos(\theta))\big|\theta\in\mathbb{R}\big\}\] and use the Pythagorean identity to justify that this is in fact the unit circle. Prove the Pythagorean identity holds for complex values as well, i.e. \(\sin^2(z)+\cos^2(z)=1\) for all \(z\in\mathbb{C}\), but show that the pair \((\sin(z),\cos(z))\) has norm 1 only when \(z\) is real. That is, prove \[|\sin(z)|^2+|\cos(z)|^2=1\iff z\in\mathbb{R}\] For extra credit, visualize the surface defined by \[w=|\sin(z)|^2+|\cos(z)|^2\] sitting in \(\mathbb{C}^2\). Is this naturally a Riemann surface?
